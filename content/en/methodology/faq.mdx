Below are answers to the most common questions we hear from teams adopting the Problem-Driven AI methodology. If you have a question not covered here, the community is the best resource—we learn from everyone.

## What if we already know the problem?

The first illusion in any project is that the problem is already understood. Teams come with a stated problem—"Our onboarding is too slow" or "Customers churn after 30 days"—convinced they know what needs to be fixed.

The Problem Phase exists because nearly every team discovers their stated problem is actually a symptom. The real problem is three layers deeper. You don't skip discovery because you think you know; you go through it precisely to validate what you think you know.

Sometimes you'll discover the stated problem was correct all along. You just earned the right to believe it through evidence, not assumption. That's worth the investment.

## How does this differ from Design Thinking?

Design Thinking is a powerful framework that excels at empathetic understanding, rapid prototyping, and iterative refinement. Problem-Driven AI incorporates those strengths but adds three critical layers: it starts before empathy (in the raw problem discovery), adds a dedicated alignment phase (Solution Phase) where the entire organization gets to consensus before building, and bridges the gap between human-centered discovery and AI-driven context engineering.

Think of it this way: Design Thinking helps you understand *what people need*. Problem-Driven AI helps you understand that, then turns it into *a precise statement of what AI needs to know* to help solve it.

## How long does the process take?

It depends entirely on the complexity of the problem space and organizational alignment. We've seen teams complete the Problem Phase in 2 weeks for a well-scoped product optimization. We've also seen healthy explorations take 8 weeks for a novel market entry.

The methodology is not a race. The metric is clarity, not velocity. If your team is moving fast but misaligned, you're about to spend months building in the wrong direction. That's expensive time. The phase durations in the planning sections give realistic guidance based on scope.

## Can we skip phases?

Technically, yes. You can skip the entire process and build with AI directly. Organizations do this every day. They also discover, three months in, that they optimized a feature nobody wanted or solved a problem that doesn't actually exist at scale.

Skipping phases is betting that your initial understanding is flawless and that organizational consensus spontaneously emerges. Neither bet is good. You will pay the cost eventually—either in shortened discovery (and cascading problems later) or in misaligned builds that nobody is enthusiastic about using.

The phases exist because they address specific, high-leverage risks. Skip them at your own expense.

## What if stakeholders disagree?

This is the entire point of the Solution Phase. Stakeholders *should* disagree. Different perspectives represent different truths about the problem, different constraints, different opportunities.

The goal is not to eliminate disagreement. It's to surface disagreement early, reason through it together, and arrive at organizational consensus *before* you build. This is uncomfortable and necessary.

When stakeholders disagree and you go to build anyway, you get teams pulling in different directions, contexts that contradict each other, and a product that satisfies no one. When stakeholders disagree and you align first, you get a coherent solution that everyone understands and owns.

## Is this only for AI products?

No. The Problem-Driven methodology is useful for any product where the quality of the initial thinking matters—which is to say, all products. But it's especially critical for AI products because AI compounds uncertainty. A poorly understood problem fed into a GPT-4 becomes a perfectly built wrong answer. At scale. In minutes.

That said, the AI Build Phase assumes you're using AI as a core tool. If you're not building with AI, you can adapt the methodology—the thinking remains the same; you just apply it in a traditional engineering context instead.

## How do we measure success?

Success is measured differently across phases:

- **Problem Phase:** You've found the real problem, not the symptom. You can articulate why this problem matters and who it matters to.
- **Solution Phase:** The organization has consensus. Everyone can articulate the same solution and why it works for the problem discovered.
- **Context Phase:** The context system is precise, unambiguous, and teams can use it to build independently without constant rewriting.
- **AI Build Phase:** The AI system does what the context specified it should do.
- **Market Phase:** Real users are using it, finding it valuable, and returning.

Each phase has different success criteria. The gate reviews at the end of each phase in the planning sections detail exactly what "done" looks like.

## What team size do we need?

The methodology works for teams of 2 and organizations of 200. The roles and activities scale. A solo founder still needs to do problem discovery—they just do it with a much smaller time investment. A large enterprise needs the same phases but with more parallel work and more stakeholders to align.

The minimum requirement is intellectual diversity—at least one person thinking like a customer, at least one thinking like a builder, and leadership that cares about alignment. Everything else scales with organizational size.
